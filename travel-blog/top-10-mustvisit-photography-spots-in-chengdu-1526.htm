Top 10 Must-Visit Photography Spots in Chengdu

  • Home
  • /
  • Travel Blog
  • /
  • Blog Details

Chengdu, the capital of Sichuan Province, is a city that effortlessly blends ancient charm with modern vibrancy. Known for its spicy cuisine, laid-back teahouse culture, and of course, the adorable giant pandas, Chengdu is also a paradise for photographers. Whether you’re a professional or an amateur, the city offers countless picturesque locations to capture stunning shots. Here are the top 10 must-visit photography spots in Chengdu that will make your Instagram feed shine.

1. Chengdu Research Base of Giant Panda Breeding

Why It’s a Must-Visit

No trip to Chengdu is complete without seeing its most famous residents—the giant pandas. The Chengdu Panda Base is not only a conservation center but also a dream location for wildlife photographers.

Best Time to Shoot

Early morning (8:00 AM - 10:00 AM) is ideal, as pandas are most active during feeding time. The soft morning light also creates a magical glow in your photos.

Photography Tips

  • Use a fast shutter speed to capture playful pandas in action.
  • Focus on close-ups of their expressive faces and paws.
  • Don’t miss the red pandas—they’re equally photogenic!

2. Jinli Ancient Street

Why It’s a Must-Visit

Jinli Street is a beautifully preserved ancient alley that dates back to the Qin Dynasty. With its red lanterns, traditional Sichuanese architecture, and bustling food stalls, it’s a goldmine for cultural photography.

Best Time to Shoot

Late afternoon or early evening when the lanterns light up, creating a warm, nostalgic atmosphere.

Photography Tips

  • Capture the contrast between old and new—vendors in traditional dress against modern tourists.
  • Shoot long exposures to blur the crowds while keeping the architecture sharp.
  • Don’t forget the street food close-ups—spicy skewers and sweet tanghulu make great subjects.

3. Wuhou Shrine (Wuhou Ci)

Why It’s a Must-Visit

This tranquil temple complex honors Zhuge Liang, a legendary strategist from the Three Kingdoms period. The lush gardens, red walls, and intricate wooden carvings offer endless compositional possibilities.

Best Time to Shoot

Weekday mornings to avoid crowds, or during golden hour for soft, dramatic lighting.

Photography Tips

  • Frame shots with the iconic red walls and green cypress trees.
  • Capture details like ancient stone tablets and calligraphy.
  • Use a polarizing filter to enhance the colors of the rooftops.

4. Kuanzhai Alley (Kuanzhai Xiangzi)

Why It’s a Must-Visit

This historic district consists of three alleys—Wide (Kuan), Narrow (Zhai), and Well (Jing)—each offering a unique blend of Qing Dynasty architecture and modern cafes.

Best Time to Shoot

Early morning for empty streets, or at night when the alleys are illuminated.

Photography Tips

  • Shoot from high angles to capture the maze-like layout.
  • Look for reflections in puddles after rain.
  • Focus on the contrast between old brick walls and trendy boutiques.

5. Chunxi Road

Why It’s a Must-Visit

Chengdu’s premier shopping district is a neon-lit wonderland at night, perfect for urban and street photography.

Best Time to Shoot

Blue hour (just after sunset) when the city lights come alive.

Photography Tips

  • Use a tripod for crisp long exposures of traffic trails.
  • Capture candid shots of shoppers and street performers.
  • Experiment with light painting techniques.

6. Qingcheng Mountain

Why It’s a Must-Visit

A UNESCO World Heritage Site, Qingcheng Mountain is one of Taoism’s birthplaces. Its misty peaks, ancient temples, and winding paths are straight out of a Chinese ink painting.

Best Time to Shoot

Sunrise or on foggy days for an ethereal vibe.

Photography Tips

  • Use a telephoto lens to compress the mountain layers.
  • Shoot waterfalls with a slow shutter speed for silky effects.
  • Include Taoist monks in your frames for storytelling.

7. Dujiangyan Irrigation System

Why It’s a Must-Visit

This 2,000-year-old engineering marvel is not only historically significant but also visually stunning, with bridges, canals, and lush surroundings.

Best Time to Shoot

Spring when the cherry blossoms bloom, or autumn for golden foliage.

Photography Tips

  • Fly a drone for aerial shots of the water dividers.
  • Capture the interplay of light and water at the Fish Mouth Levee.
  • Include local farmers for a human element.

8. Sichuan Opera Face-Changing Performances

Why It’s a Must-Visit

The rapid “face-changing” (Bian Lian) performances are a Sichuan specialty. The vibrant masks and dramatic movements are a feast for the lens.

Best Time to Shoot

During evening shows at Shufeng Yayun Teahouse.

Photography Tips

  • Use burst mode to catch the split-second mask changes.
  • Focus on the performers’ eyes for emotional impact.
  • Shoot in high ISO to freeze motion under dim stage lights.

9. Dongjiao Memory (Dongjiao Ji Yi)

Why It’s a Must-Visit

This creative arts district, set in a former factory, is Chengdu’s answer to Beijing’s 798. Graffiti, industrial relics, and hipster cafes abound.

Best Time to Shoot

Weekends when the area is liveliest, but weekdays for minimalist shots.

Photography Tips

  • Play with leading lines from old railway tracks.
  • Shoot black-and-white for a gritty, retro feel.
  • Frame artists at work in their studios.

10. Anshun Bridge (Anshun Langqiao)

Why It’s a Must-Visit

This historic bridge over the Jin River offers panoramic views of Chengdu’s skyline, especially when lit at night.

Best Time to Shoot

Twilight for a mix of natural and artificial light.

Photography Tips

  • Use a wide-angle lens to include the river reflections.
  • Experiment with symmetry by centering the bridge.
  • Capture the LED light shows on nearby buildings.

Each of these spots tells a different story of Chengdu—from its ancient roots to its dynamic present. Pack your camera, charge your batteries, and get ready to explore the visual wonders of this unforgettable city.
